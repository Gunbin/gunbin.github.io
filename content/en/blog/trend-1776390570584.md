## The Science: Why Your Digestion Speed Changes Everything

When you start on medications like **Ozempic** or **Zepbound**, the **GLP-1** receptor agonists go to work by mimicking a hormone that slows down gastric emptying. In plain English: food and pills sit in your stomach much longer than they used to. This is great for feeling full, but it’s a nightmare for traditional supplement absorption. 

Normally, a vitamin hits your stomach, dissolves, and moves into the small intestine for absorption. On these meds, that vitamin might sit in a pool of stomach acid for hours longer than intended. This delayed process can lead to the degradation of the supplement before it even works, or worse, it can irritate the stomach lining, leading to localized inflammation and that "brick in my stomach" feeling.

## The 'Dangerous 5': Supplements That Clash with Your Meds

Not every supplement is a villain, but these five categories are frequently identified by medical experts as high-risk for anyone on a **GLP-1** regimen.

| Supplement Category | The Specific Risk | Resulting Symptom |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **High-Fiber (Psyllium)** | Absorbs water and expands in a slow-moving gut. | Severe bloating and potential bowel obstruction. |
| **Bulk Minerals (Iron/Zinc)** | Known to be heavy on the stomach; stay in the gut too long. | Intense **gastrointestinal distress** and metallic reflux. |
| **St. John’s Wort** | Alters liver enzymes responsible for drug metabolism. | Can decrease the effectiveness of your primary medication. |
| **High-Dose Vitamin C** | Increased acidity in a slow-emptying stomach. | Acid reflux and "sulfur burps." |
| **Herbal Laxatives** | Can cause unpredictable cramping when combined with slowed motility. | Dehydration and electrolyte imbalance. |

## Why Specific Vitamins and Minerals Are Now Causing Nausea

You might have taken a daily multivitamin for years without an issue, only to find that it now makes you want to crawl into a hole. This happens because **Vitamins and Minerals**—specifically fat-soluble ones like A, D, E, and K—require specific conditions to be absorbed correctly. 

When your digestion slows, the concentration of these substances stays high in the stomach for an extended period. High concentrations of iron or zinc are notorious for triggering the "nausea center" in the brain when they don't move into the intestine quickly enough. If you’re experiencing sudden waves of sickness an hour after your morning routine, your "healthy" supplements are likely the culprit.

## How to Audit Your Cabinet: Your Step-by-Step Action Plan

I hate seeing people suffer through "side effects" that are actually just bad drug interactions. If you want to keep your results and lose the nausea, you need to be proactive. Spend some time this weekend doing a "supplement triage."

1.  **The "Empty Stomach" Test:** Check every label. If a supplement says "take with food," it’s because it’s an irritant. On a **GLP-1**, "with food" means it’s going to sit there even longer. Consider pausing these first.
2.  **Switch to Liquids or Sublinguals:** Because gastric emptying is the bottleneck, vitamins that absorb under the tongue (sublingual) or liquids bypass the stomach transit issue entirely. This is a game-changer for B12 and Vitamin D.
3.  **Check for Hidden Fillers:** Many cheap supplements use sugar alcohols (like sorbitol or xylitol) or lactose as fillers. In a slow gut, these can ferment, causing the dreaded "Ozempic burps" and gas.
4.  **Prioritize Electrolytes Over Bulky Multi-Vitamins:** Most people on **Ozempic** or **Zepbound** don't need a massive multi; they need magnesium, potassium, and sodium to stay hydrated. Focus on clean electrolyte powders without artificial sweeteners.

## Your Refill Checklist: Questions for Your Doctor

Don’t just assume your doctor knows every "natural" gummy you’re chewing. At your next refill appointment, bring your actual bottles with you. Do not just bring a list; show them the ingredients. 

Ask these three specific questions:
*   "Given my current rate of weight loss, are my dosages of fat-soluble **Vitamins and Minerals** still appropriate, or am I at risk for toxicity?"
*   "Can we test my actual levels of B12 and Vitamin D before I continue these supplements?"
*   "Are any of these specific binders or fillers known to aggravate **gastrointestinal distress** in patients with delayed gastric emptying?"

## The Future of Biohacking in the Age of GLP-1s

We are entering a new era of metabolic health where the "more is better" approach to supplementation is officially dead. The trend of taking 20 different pills to "optimize" your health doesn't work when your internal plumbing has changed its schedule. 

Are we eventually going to see supplements specifically formulated for slowed digestion? Probably. But until then, the smartest move you can make is to simplify. If a supplement isn't solving a lab-proven deficiency, it’s likely just adding to the load your stomach is already struggling to handle. Stop being a guinea pig for the supplement industry and start listening to what your gut is actually telling you.
